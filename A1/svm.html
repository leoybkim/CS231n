Inline Question 1:¶

It is possible that once in a while a dimension in the gradcheck will not match exactly. What could such a discrepancy be caused by? Is it a reason for concern? What is a simple example in one dimension where a gradient check could fail? Hint: the SVM loss function is not strictly speaking differentiable

Your Answer: SVM loss function is not differentiable at the very hinge. For example, f(x) = max(0, x) where x=0. There is no gradient at that point. It is not a reason for concern since we can approximate it by evaluating a very small number above 0 and very small number below 0.

Describe what your visualized SVM weights look like, and offer a brief explanation for why they look they way that they do.

Your answer: The visulized SVM weights have some resemblance to the image of their ground truth label. For example, frogs are generally green and we see the green blob in the center of the image. Ships are normally in water and the general background the visulization of ships's SVM weights are blue. This is because each of them have been trained to like the images of the given class in terms of RGB pixel values and its location on the image. So the overall outcome looks like an average pixels values of that given class, largely based on their colours and the location of that coloured pixel on the image. The overall 'template' may not be intuitive. For example, the SVM weights of horse shows two head pointing at either direction of the image. Horse should have one head not two. This is because the training sets contained both versions of horses and the trained results ended up merging both variations.
